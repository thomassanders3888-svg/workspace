This isn't a trend. This is a civilization-level event.

### 2/13

"But overpopulation!"

We've heard this for 50 years. Wrong problem. Completely inverted.

The UN projects global fertility will fall below replacement by 2050. Peak population: ~9 billion, then rapid decline. We're building infrastructure for a future that won't exist.

---

### 3/13

Why should you care?

Simple: demographic pyramid inversion.

When old people outnumber young:
- Pension systems collapse (math: 2 workers per retiree, not sustainable)
- Property values crater (supply/demand)
- Healthcare systems drown
- Innovation slows (young people start companies)

---

### 4/13

The economic model we've built assumes one thing: growth.

Not efficiency. Not sustainability. Growth.

More consumers. More workers. More taxpayers next year than this year.

What happens when the denominator shrinks? To everything?

---

### 5/13

Three mechanisms are driving this:

1) **Education-wealth-fertility tradeoff.** Women with education and career options delay/forego children. (Not blame. Just mechanics.)

2) **Cost disease.** Housing, education, healthcare prices grew 2-3x faster than wages since 1970.

3) **Cultural shift.** Kids went from economic asset (farm labor) to economic liability (cost center).

---

### 6/13

Here's the uncomfortable truth:

Every fix is politically impossible.

- Immigration? Taboo in most wealthy nations.
- Child subsidies? Expensive and marginally effective.
- Return to traditional gender roles? Society chose differently.
- AI/automation replacing workers? Solves productivity, not tax base.

### 7/13

Some countries are trying:

- Hungary: 4% GDP on pro-natalist policy ($35k loans for 3 kids, tax breaks). Result: 1.59 fertility. Still below replacement.

- Japan: Massive spending. Result: 1.26.

- Singapore: Cash for babies. Result: 1.04.

Nothing works at scale.
